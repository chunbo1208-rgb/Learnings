In [electrochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemistry "Electrochemistry"), **standard electrode potential** E⊖![{\displaystyle E^{\ominus }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d79e73b6c90d0170701b9e47deb3bda880d90584), or Ered⊖![{\displaystyle E_{red}^{\ominus }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2eca8e3be4f894ae5145e50114c550b2b2df77fa), is the [electrode potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrode_potential "Electrode potential") (a measure of the reducing power of any element or compound) which the IUPAC "Gold Book" defines as _"the value of the standard [emf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromotive_force "Electromotive force") ([electromotive force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromotive_force "Electromotive force")) of a cell in which molecular hydrogen under [standard pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_pressure "Standard pressure") is oxidized to solvated protons at the left-hand electrode"_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential#cite_note-1)

| Half-Reaction                   | E^\circ (Volts) | Oxidizing Strength |
| ------------------------------- | --------------- | ------------------ |
| $\ce{Fe^{3+} + e^- -> Fe^{2+}}$ | +0.77 V         | Strongest          |
| $\ce{S + 2H^+ + 2e^- -> H2S}$   | +0.14 V         | Middle             |
| $\ce{Fe^{2+} + 2e^- -> Fe}$     | -0.44 V         | Weakest            |
